Part of series: Burnout

Why your best engineers are fading (and you might be next)

27 December 2025 / 8 min read
  • leadership
  • burnout
  • management

The engineer you’re most worried about losing isn’t the one complaining loudly. It’s the one who stopped complaining.

Six months ago, they were in every design discussion. They pushed back on bad ideas. They stayed late not because anyone asked, but because they cared about getting it right. They were the person you pointed new hires toward: “Watch how they work.”

Now they’re quiet. They hit their commitments, but barely. They stopped volunteering for the hard problems. In meetings, they’re present but not engaged. When you ask for their opinion, you get “whatever the team decides.”

They’re not struggling. They’re fading.

And if you don’t catch it now, you’ll lose them. Not to a competitor—though that might happen too—but to the version of themselves that stopped caring. That’s the real loss.

The high performer paradox

Burnout disproportionately hits your best people. Not the coasters. Not the ones doing the minimum. The ones who give a damn.

The logic is simple. Burnout happens when the investment-to-return ratio breaks down. When you put in effort and it doesn’t matter. When you care about outcomes and outcomes don’t improve. When you push hard and nothing changes.

People who don’t invest much can’t experience that mismatch. They’re not putting in enough to feel the gap. But your high performers? They’re all-in. They’ve staked their identity on doing excellent work. When excellence stops paying off—when the codebase stays broken, when leadership ignores their input, when the roadmap changes again—they feel it acutely.

The ones who care the most have the most to lose when caring stops working.

The yield gap

Every engineer has an internal ledger. It’s not conscious, but it’s always running. Effort in, results out. When the ratio is healthy, work feels sustainable. When the ratio breaks, burnout follows.

The gap shows up in different ways:

The shipped feature nobody uses. They spent three months building it. The product team moved on to the next thing before adoption even started. The code sits there, technically working, practically pointless. You can only do this a few times before something breaks.

The refactor that got reverted. They fixed the technical debt. They made the codebase better. Then a reorg happened, new leadership came in, and they decided to rewrite everything in a different framework. Their work didn’t just not matter—it was actively unwound.

The on-call rotation that never improves. They document the runbooks. They fix the root causes. They propose solutions to the systemic issues. Nothing changes. The same alerts fire. The same problems recur. Their effort disappears into the void.

The promotion that didn’t happen. They delivered. They mentored. They led the critical project. The feedback was positive. And then someone more visible got the slot, or the level was eliminated, or the goalposts moved. The message: effort doesn’t map to advancement.

The feedback that got ignored. They raised the concern in the design review. They wrote the RFC explaining the risk. They were right—the problem they predicted happened exactly as they said. And nobody acknowledged it. They just moved on to the next fire.

Any of these in isolation is manageable. But they compound. Each one depletes the reserve of belief that effort matters. After enough of them, high performers stop performing. Not because they can’t—because they’ve learned it doesn’t help.

What fading looks like

If you manage people, you need to recognize this early. Here’s what to watch for:

The short fuse. They used to be patient with process. Now small inefficiencies set them off. Code reviews become curt. Feedback that used to be constructive becomes dismissive. Their tolerance for friction has collapsed.

The preemptive dismissal. “We’ve tried this before.” “That’ll never work here.” “Leadership will just change their mind.” They’re not analyzing proposals—they’re protecting themselves from investing in something that will be abandoned.

The meeting dread. Watch for the body language shift when you schedule a 1:1. Listen for the sighs when another “quick sync” gets added to the calendar. When someone starts treating every meeting as an interruption rather than collaboration, something has broken.

The withdrawal from extras. They stop mentoring. They skip the optional team events. They’re still doing their job, but only their job. The discretionary effort that used to flow naturally has dried up.

The sarcasm escalation. Humor can be healthy. But when every observation becomes sardonic, when “we should probably document this” becomes “sure, that’ll definitely get read”—that’s not wit. That’s armor.

The expertise hoarding. They used to share knowledge freely. Now getting information from them is like pulling teeth. Why invest in making others better when nothing you do seems to matter?

The maturity mirage. This is the dangerous one. They’re less combative. They’re more “collaborative.” They’ve stopped being “difficult.” Managers sometimes celebrate this transition. “They’ve really mellowed out.” No. They’ve given up. You just can’t tell the difference.

None of these are personality traits. They’re behaviors that emerge when someone has decided—consciously or not—that protecting themselves is more important than engaging fully.

The “just work less” trap

When managers notice someone struggling, the instinctive advice is: work fewer hours. Take a vacation. Set better boundaries.

This advice misses the point.

Burnout isn’t primarily about hours. It’s about the relationship between effort and meaning. You can burn out working 40 hours a week if those hours feel pointless. You can work 60 hours and feel energized if the work matters and you have autonomy over how it’s done.

Telling a burned-out high performer to work less is like telling someone in a bad relationship to spend fewer hours with their partner. It might reduce the immediate pain, but it doesn’t address why the relationship is broken.

Working less in a broken environment just means burning out slower.

What you can actually do

If you’re watching someone fade, here’s what works better than suggesting vacation:

Ask about the ratio. “Where are you putting in effort that isn’t paying off?” is a better question than “how are you doing?” It cuts to the actual problem.

Investigate the yield gap. What specifically has broken down? Is it workload? Control? Recognition? Fairness? You can’t fix what you haven’t diagnosed. And they might not volunteer the information unless you ask directly.

Fix one thing they’ve complained about. Not a big thing, necessarily. But something that shows their feedback matters. That speaking up leads to change. Rebuild the belief that effort yields results.

Make their impact visible. High performers often suffer from success invisibility—they do great work, it becomes expected, and no one notices anymore. Fight this. Point out what they’ve accomplished. Make sure leadership knows. Recognition doesn’t cost anything.

Shield them from the chaos. If the roadmap keeps changing, absorb some of that instability. Don’t pass every pivot directly to the team. Create pockets of stability where good work can compound.

Give them control. Often the issue isn’t workload but autonomy. Can they choose how to solve the problem? Can they influence what problems get solved? The more ownership they have, the more investment makes sense.

Have the real conversation. Not “you seem stressed” but “I’ve noticed you seem less engaged lately. If I’m reading that right, I want to understand what’s going on. What would need to change for you to feel good about the work?”

Then actually listen. And act on what you hear.

Why you might be next

If you’re reading this as a manager, here’s the uncomfortable part: you’re at higher risk than your reports.

The same dynamics that burn out high performers apply to you, amplified. You care about your team’s outcomes. You invest in people. You fight battles on their behalf. You absorb the chaos from above so they can focus.

And what do you get? More responsibility. Tighter timelines. Less control. Accountability in both directions—blamed when things fail, invisible when things succeed.

The yield gap is even starker for managers. You can do everything right and still lose people. Still miss targets. Still get reorganized out of existence. The variables that determine your success are largely outside your control.

If you’ve noticed yourself becoming cynical about leadership. If you dread your own 1:1s with your manager. If you’ve stopped advocating for things because “it won’t change anything.” If you’re exhausted in a way that weekends don’t fix.

You’re not immune. You might already be fading.

The real loss

When a high performer burns out and leaves, you lose their output. That’s visible. That’s what the org notices.

But the real loss is harder to see. You lose the standard they set for everyone else. You lose the mentorship they provided. You lose the institutional knowledge they carried. You lose the person who cared enough to push back when things were going wrong.

And you lose them to a version of themselves that’s diminished. Wherever they go next—if they go—they’ll be starting from a depleted state. Rebuilding their belief that caring is worth it. Hoping the next place won’t teach them the same lesson.

That’s the cost of not catching the fade. Not just losing an employee. Losing who they were when they still believed the work mattered.

Questions worth sitting with:

  • Who on your team has gotten quieter lately? What might that mean?
  • Where has the yield gap opened up for your best people?
  • What would it take to make effort feel worth it again—for them, and for you?
