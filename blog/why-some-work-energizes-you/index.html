Why some work energizes you (a framework for tech leaders)

27 December 2025 / 9 min read
  • leadership
  • burnout
  • productivity

Here’s something that took me years to figure out: an hour of work is not an hour of work.

Some hours leave me energized. I finish a deep coding session or a productive design discussion and I feel more capable than when I started. The work generated energy rather than consuming it.

Other hours leave me drained. An hour of context-switching between Slack threads, or a poorly-run meeting that could have been an email, or a performance review that went nowhere—these don’t just use energy. They put me in a deficit.

If you treat all work as equivalent and just try to “work less,” you’ll optimize for the wrong thing. The goal isn’t fewer hours. It’s a better ratio of generative work to draining work. Get that ratio right, and you can work more sustainably than someone working half your hours on the wrong things.

The three types of work energy

I’ve started thinking about work in three categories, based on how they affect my energy over time.

Generative work creates energy. You finish and feel more capable, more engaged, more alive. For me, this includes deep problem-solving, writing that’s going well, architecture discussions with people I respect, and mentoring sessions where something clicks for the other person.

The key feature of generative work: it compounds. You get better at it. The investment pays off. You can see your progress. It connects to something you care about.

Neutral work consumes energy proportionally. It takes effort, but it doesn’t leave you depleted. Routine code reviews. Status updates. Well-run meetings with clear agendas. Admin tasks that you understand and can complete efficiently.

Neutral work is fine. You can do a lot of it without burning out, as long as it’s balanced by generative work.

Draining work consumes more energy than its duration suggests. An hour of draining work can cost you two hours of capacity. You finish and feel worse. Your motivation drops. Your patience shrinks. You start dreading the rest of your day.

For me, draining work includes: unnecessary meetings, context-switching between unrelated problems, performative activities (looking busy vs. being productive), conflict that goes nowhere, and work that feels pointless or doomed to be ignored.

The ratio matters more than the total. A week that’s 40% generative, 50% neutral, and 10% draining is sustainable. A week that’s 10% generative and 40% draining will grind you down even if the total hours are lower.

The creative energy battery

Here’s a model I’ve found useful: think of creative capacity as a battery, not a tank.

A tank holds a fixed amount. Use it up, and you need to refill it. The implication is that you can binge—use a lot at once, then refill all at once.

A battery works differently. It has a maximum charge rate. You can’t binge-charge it. If you drain it completely, it takes time to recover, and you can’t accelerate that by just “resting harder.”

Creative work operates like a battery. You have a daily creative capacity—maybe 3-4 hours of truly deep work. You can’t do 8 hours today and 0 tomorrow and call it even. The capacity regenerates at its own pace.

This has implications:

You can’t bank creative energy. Taking a vacation doesn’t let you come back and do 12-hour creative days. The battery still charges at the same rate.

Depleting the battery regularly is worse than running it low. If you consistently drain yourself to zero, recovery gets slower. The battery degrades. People who push through exhaustion for months find that their capacity has permanently shrunk.

Short sessions can be more productive than long ones. Two 2-hour blocks of deep work, with recovery in between, often produce more than one 5-hour block where you’re depleted for the last two hours.

Draining work depletes the battery even though it’s not creative. A morning of pointless meetings doesn’t just use your meeting energy—it drains your creative battery too. You can’t context-switch to deep work and expect the battery to be full.

Mental composting

There’s another phenomenon that took me too long to recognize: ideas improve when you stop working on them.

I call it mental composting. You work on a problem, get stuck, walk away—and when you come back, something has shifted. The solution is clearer. Connections you didn’t see before are obvious now. The work you did before leaving was necessary, but the breakthrough happened while you weren’t working.

This is real. It’s not laziness. It’s how complex problem-solving works. Your brain continues processing in the background. The spacing effect in learning applies to creative work too.

The implication: intense focus sessions need to be punctuated by time away. Working on something for 8 hours straight is often less effective than working 3 hours, taking a complete break, and coming back for 2 more. The break isn’t wasted time—it’s when the composting happens.

I’ve started structuring my week around this. Complex problems get multiple short sessions spread across days, not marathon sessions trying to force completion. The work goes slower in clock time but faster in outcome time.

The paradox of working more days

Here’s something counterintuitive: sometimes working more days per week leads to less burnout.

This sounds wrong. Shouldn’t fewer working days mean more rest?

It depends on what those days look like.

If you compress five days of work into four, you don’t get a three-day weekend—you get four days of intense pressure with no buffer. Every day is a gauntlet. There’s no room for the neutral work that gives you space to breathe. There’s no slack for composting time. You’re running at full intensity, and by Friday (now Thursday), you’re destroyed.

Conversely, if you spread the same work across five or six days, each day has more margin. You can afford a slow morning. You can take a real lunch. You can end at a reasonable hour because tomorrow is also a workday. The intensity per day drops, and the recovery happens in small increments throughout the week rather than in one desperate weekend.

I’ve experimented with this. My most sustainable periods have been when I worked six moderate days, not five intense ones. The total hours were similar. The experience was completely different.

This doesn’t mean more days is always better—you need actual rest too. But the relationship between days worked and burnout is not linear. Intensity matters more than duration.

Designing your week

If you buy this framework, the practical question is: how do you structure your time to maximize generative work and minimize draining work?

Some tactics I’ve found useful:

Audit your calendar for energy, not just time. Go through last week’s meetings. Mark each one as generative, neutral, or draining. Calculate the ratio. If draining work is above 20%, something is wrong. Look for patterns—is it specific meetings? Specific times of day? Specific people?

Protect blocks for generative work. Don’t let meetings fill your calendar uniformly. Create blocks where deep work is possible and defend them. A fragmented day with no block longer than 45 minutes cannot contain generative work.

Batch the draining stuff. If certain activities drain you but can’t be eliminated, batch them together. One afternoon of annoying meetings is better than five days where each day is interrupted by an annoying meeting. The dread and context-switching are almost as draining as the activity itself.

Sequence matters. Don’t schedule your most draining meeting right before your most important work. Put neutral work after draining work as a buffer. Put generative work early in the day before your battery depletes.

Build in composting time. If you’re working on something hard, don’t schedule it back-to-back. Leave gaps. Work on it Monday morning, leave it alone, return Wednesday. Trust that your brain is doing something useful in between.

Know your recharge activities. What do you do during breaks that actually replenishes energy? For some people it’s walking. For others it’s reading something unrelated to work. For others it’s a nap. Whatever it is, make sure it’s actually available during your day, not crowded out by Slack notifications.

What this means for leaders

If you manage people, this framework has implications beyond your own schedule.

Your reports have the same energy dynamics you do. They have generative work, neutral work, and draining work. They have creative batteries that can be depleted. They need composting time.

When you add a meeting to someone’s calendar, you’re not just taking 30 minutes. You’re potentially disrupting their generative blocks. You’re adding context-switching. You might be depleting their creative capacity for the rest of the day.

When you create a culture of constant availability—Slack messages that expect immediate responses, meetings scheduled with no notice, an expectation that everyone is always interruptible—you’re creating an environment where generative work is impossible. You’re forcing everyone into fragmented, draining days.

The most productive teams I’ve been on were not the ones that worked the most hours. They were the ones that protected time for deep work, batched interruptions, and accepted that some things could wait.

You can build this culture intentionally. Establish norms around when people need to be responsive and when they can go heads-down. Default meetings to 25 or 50 minutes to create buffer. Cancel recurring meetings that aren’t serving a purpose. Ask, before scheduling something, whether this meeting is generative, neutral, or draining for the people attending.

The real anti-burnout strategy

Most advice about avoiding burnout focuses on doing less. Work fewer hours. Take more vacations. Set boundaries.

This advice isn’t wrong, but it’s incomplete. The goal isn’t to work less. It’s to work in a way that’s sustainable.

Sustainable work means a healthy ratio of generative to draining activities. It means respecting your creative battery’s charge rate. It means building in composting time. It means understanding that intensity matters more than duration.

Get these right, and you can work more hours with less burnout. Get them wrong, and you can work fewer hours and still burn out.

The question isn’t “how much are you working?” It’s “how does your work make you feel?” If the answer is “depleted,” no amount of weekends will fix it. You need to change what the work looks like, not just how much of it you do.

Questions worth sitting with:

  • What work have you done recently that left you more energized than when you started?
  • What percentage of your week is draining work? Is that sustainable?
  • When was the last time you had real composting time—space for your mind to process a problem without actively working on it?
