Let's talk about choosing a database in 2026

31 March 2026 / 42 min read
  • databases
  • postgresql
  • architecture

I read Making Sense of NoSQL by Dan McCreary and Ann Kelly when Manning published it in 2013 (Yeah! That old). The book was a faithful snapshot of the prevailing wisdom:

relational databases were cracking under the pressure of modern workloads, and a new generation of distributed, schema-free, join-free databases would save us.

The four horsemen of the RDBMS apocalypse were Volume, Velocity, Variability, and Agility.

It’s 2026. Thirteen years later, I find myself looking back with the clarity that only hindsight provides. The mantra stuck, though. I still hear engineers repeating these limitations as if nothing has changed.

PostgreSQL became the default choice for most new applications. Not because relational databases were perfect in 2014 (they weren’t), but because the PostgreSQL community addressed every legitimate criticism the NoSQL movement raised, one by one. Meanwhile, the NoSQL ecosystem fragmented, consolidated, and in many cases quietly re-implemented the relational features it once mocked. I’ve watched this play out across multiple teams and companies.

This isn’t a polemic against all non-relational databases. Purpose-built systems exist for good reasons, and I’ll be specific about when they earn their place. But it is a direct challenge to the decision-making framework that an entire generation of engineers internalized: the idea that choosing a database starts with asking “relational or NoSQL?” In 2026, that question is plainly wrong.

Let’s talk about what actually matters.

Fair warning: this is a long one. Bookmark it, come back between sections, absorb it at your own pace.

Part I: the original NoSQL thesis

The four business drivers

Making Sense of NoSQL identifies four forces that push organizations away from traditional RDBMSs:

  • Volume - Datasets too large for a single machine.
  • Velocity - Read/write throughput that exceeds what a single-processor RDBMS can deliver.
  • Variability - Data that doesn’t fit neatly into predefined table schemas.
  • Agility - The ability to rapidly build and modify applications without being bottlenecked by database schema changes.

McCreary and Kelly frame these as existential threats to the relational model. The implicit argument: if your system needs any of these properties, you need to leave the relational world behind and adopt one of the four NoSQL patterns (key-value stores, document stores, column-family stores, or graph databases).

This framing was seductive. It gave engineers and managers a clean taxonomy: identify your primary driver, pick the corresponding NoSQL category, select a product. But the taxonomy was wrong, because it confused the limitations of specific RDBMS implementations circa 2010 with fundamental limitations of the relational model.

That conflation (between the relational model and a particular generation of its implementations) is the core error of the NoSQL movement. Every argument that follows in the community rests on it.

The ACID vs. BASE binary

The book presents ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) and BASE (Basically Available, Soft-state, Eventually consistent) as two competing transaction models. ACID is described as pessimistic, expensive, and associated with single-node relational databases. BASE is described as optimistic, cheaper, and the natural fit for distributed systems.

The text includes a memorable figure comparing the two approaches: ACID is illustrated with a stressed businessman in a suit; BASE with someone relaxing in a hammock on a beach. The visual metaphor is clear: ACID is uptight and expensive; BASE is relaxed and efficient.

This framing has a critical flaw: it presents consistency as a luxury you pay for, rather than a correctness property you give up at your risk. To me, it’s the equivalent of presenting seatbelts as an optional luxury that slows down your car.

The CAP theorem as decision framework

The 2013 text correctly explains Brewer’s CAP theorem: in the presence of a network partition, a distributed system must choose between consistency and availability. But it then uses CAP as a database selection tool, implying that if you need availability, you should choose a NoSQL system that favors availability over consistency.

This overloads the CAP theorem with a purpose it was never designed for. As Eric Brewer himself clarified in CAP Twelve Years Later, CAP is about a narrow set of extreme failure scenarios, not about everyday system design. The vast majority of database operations happen when the network is healthy, and the relevant trade-offs are about latency, throughput, and consistency guarantees during normal operation, none of which CAP addresses. I’ve seen teams pick databases based on a CAP diagram on a whiteboard, and it rarely ends well.

The four data architecture patterns

the NoSQL taxonomy that became industry gospel:

PatternDescriptionExample Products
Key-value storeSimple put/get/delete by keyRedis, Riak, DynamoDB
Graph storeNodes, relationships, and propertiesNeo4j, AllegroGraph
Column-family storeSparse matrix with row and column keysCassandra, HBase
Document storeHierarchical JSON/XML structuresMongoDB, CouchDB

The taxonomy is useful as a mental model, but people treat these as mutually exclusive categories that compete with relational databases. The reality in 2026 is that PostgreSQL implements meaningful subsets of three of these four patterns (key-value, document, and basic graph traversal) within a single system, and does so with transactional guarantees that no NoSQL system in the original taxonomy could match.

Part II: the claims, examined one by one

Claim 1: “RDBMSs have rigid schemas that slow down development”

The argument: Adding new columns to an RDBMS requires the system to be shut down for ALTER TABLE commands. When a database is large, this process can impact system availability. NoSQL systems allow you to drag-and-drop your data into a folder and query it without creating an entity-relational model.

The reality in 2026:

This claim was already partially false when the book was published. PostgreSQL 9.4, released in late 2014, introduced jsonb, a binary JSON type with indexing support. But even before that, PostgreSQL had supported hstore (key-value pairs) and the xml type since 8.3 (2008).

In PostgreSQL 17, the schema flexibility story is complete:

-- Store structured and unstructured data in the same table
CREATE TABLE orders (
    id          bigint GENERATED ALWAYS AS IDENTITY PRIMARY KEY,
    customer_id bigint NOT NULL REFERENCES customers(id),
    total       numeric(10,2) NOT NULL,
    status      text NOT NULL DEFAULT 'pending',
    metadata    jsonb NOT NULL DEFAULT '{}',
    created_at  timestamptz NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);

-- Index specific JSON paths for fast queries
CREATE INDEX idx_orders_metadata_source
    ON orders USING gin ((metadata -> 'source'));

-- Query JSON fields with full SQL power
SELECT customer_id, total, metadata ->> 'campaign' AS campaign
FROM orders
WHERE metadata @> '{"source": "mobile"}'
  AND total > 100
  AND created_at > now() - interval '7 days';

This is not a compromise. You get:

  • Relational rigor where you want it: typed columns, foreign keys, constraints, CHECK clauses.
  • Schema flexibility where you need it: jsonb columns for variable attributes, user preferences, event payloads, third-party data.
  • Indexing on both: B-tree indexes on typed columns, GIN indexes on JSON paths, partial indexes combining both.
  • Transactional guarantees across all of it: A single BEGIN/COMMIT wraps updates to both the relational and document portions of your data.

Furthermore, modern PostgreSQL handles ALTER TABLE ADD COLUMN with a non-volatile default as a metadata-only operation: no table rewrite, no downtime. (Adding NOT NULL columns without defaults or generated columns can still require table rewrites on large tables.) The book’s claim that adding columns requires shutting down the system was outdated even for PostgreSQL 11 (2018), which introduced this optimization.

The deeper point: schema flexibility is not a database architecture concern; it’s a data modeling choice. PostgreSQL lets you make that choice per-column, per-table, rather than forcing an all-or-nothing commitment at the database engine level. You can have a customers table with strict relational integrity and a metadata jsonb column on the same row. No NoSQL database lets you do the reverse, adding relational constraints to a subset of your document fields with referential integrity enforcement.

In my experience, this one is fully debunked. PostgreSQL’s hybrid relational+document model is strictly more expressive than either pure relational or pure document approaches. The “rigid schema” criticism applies to Oracle circa 2005, not to PostgreSQL in 2026.

Claim 2: “RDBMSs don’t scale out; You need NoSQL for horizontal scalability”

The argument: The key factor pushing organizations to alternatives is the need to query big data using clusters of commodity processors. RDBMSs don’t support automatic sharding. When you run out of disk space, you must buy a new system and manually migrate data.

The reality in 2026:

This claim conflates three separate scaling challenges that deserve independent analysis.

Challenge A: Read scaling. PostgreSQL has supported streaming replication with hot standby since version 9.0 (2010). In 2026, setting up read replicas is a managed-service checkbox on every major cloud provider. Logical replication (available since PostgreSQL 10) allows selective table replication for more sophisticated topologies. This was a solved problem before the book was published.

Challenge B: Storage scaling. The book envisions scenarios where disk space runs out and tables must be manually migrated. PostgreSQL’s native declarative partitioning (introduced in PostgreSQL 10, significantly improved through 17) handles this:

-- Partition by time range -- the most common scaling pattern
CREATE TABLE events (
    id          bigint GENERATED ALWAYS AS IDENTITY,
    tenant_id   bigint NOT NULL,
    event_type  text NOT NULL,
    payload     jsonb NOT NULL,
    created_at  timestamptz NOT NULL
) PARTITION BY RANGE (created_at);

-- Create partitions per month
CREATE TABLE events_2026_01 PARTITION OF events
    FOR VALUES FROM ('2026-01-01') TO ('2026-02-01');

CREATE TABLE events_2026_02 PARTITION OF events
    FOR VALUES FROM ('2026-02-01') TO ('2026-03-01');

-- Queries automatically route to relevant partitions
-- Old partitions can be detached and archived without downtime

With pg_partman, partition creation and maintenance is fully automated. A single PostgreSQL instance on modern hardware (NVMe SSDs, 256GB+ RAM) comfortably handles multi-terabyte datasets.

Challenge C: Write scaling (true sharding). This is where the book’s argument had the most merit in 2014. Native PostgreSQL does not shard writes across multiple nodes. But the ecosystem in 2026 provides several mature options:

  • Citus (open source, also available as Azure Cosmos DB for PostgreSQL): Transparent distributed SQL on PostgreSQL. Shards tables across worker nodes, pushes queries down to shards, handles distributed joins and transactions.
  • Application-level sharding: For high-write workloads, sharding by tenant_id or customer_id at the application level is straightforward and well-understood. It’s not automatic, but it gives you full control over data placement.
  • Partitioning across tablespaces: For I/O-bound workloads, spreading partitions across multiple NVMe drives provides substantial throughput improvements without leaving a single node.

More importantly: most applications never need write sharding. A single PostgreSQL instance on modern hardware can sustain thousands to tens of thousands of write transactions per second. The book’s scenarios (Google Bigtable indexing the entire web, Amazon Dynamo handling Black Friday at planetary scale) are genuinely extraordinary workloads. For the other 99.9% of applications, vertical scaling with PostgreSQL is simpler, cheaper, and more reliable than distributed NoSQL.

The real question is: “At what write throughput does my application actually need to shard, and will I reach that threshold before my company either fails or can afford to solve the problem?” For most startups, a single well-tuned PostgreSQL node will carry them well past product-market fit and into scale-up territory. Instagram ran on PostgreSQL with a small engineering team, adding sharding only as growth demanded it.

But I have 100TB of data and need multi-region writes.

Then you don’t want NoSQL either. You want NewSQL: CockroachDB, YugabyteDB, Google Spanner, or TiDB. These systems provide horizontal scalability with full SQL support, distributed ACID transactions, and strong consistency. They exist precisely because the industry learned that giving up consistency (the NoSQL approach) creates more problems than it solves (shocking! I know).

The NewSQL movement is, in many ways, the industry’s admission that the NoSQL thesis was wrong about the model even when it was right about the scalability limitations of specific implementations.

I’ve watched this claim age poorly. It was valid for a narrow range of extreme-scale workloads and a specific moment in RDBMS implementation history. In 2026, between native PostgreSQL capabilities, Citus, and NewSQL systems, horizontal scalability no longer requires abandoning SQL or ACID.

Claim 3: “ACID is expensive. BASE gives you better performance and availability”

The argument: ACID systems focus on consistency and integrity above all other considerations. They are pessimistic, use lots of locks, and block reports during transactions. BASE systems are optimistic, never block writes, and assume eventual consistency. BASE systems are simpler, faster, and ideal for web storefronts.

The reality in 2026:

The book’s characterization of ACID transactions (heavy locking, blocking reads, pessimistic everything) describes Oracle’s default behavior in the 1990s, not PostgreSQL in any decade.

PostgreSQL uses Multi-Version Concurrency Control (MVCC), which means:

  • Readers never block writers. A SELECT never acquires a lock that would block an INSERT, UPDATE, or DELETE.
  • Writers never block readers. An UPDATE creates a new version of the row; concurrent SELECTs continue reading the old version until the transaction commits.
  • Only concurrent writes to the same row create contention. And even then, PostgreSQL detects conflicts and resolves them cleanly with serialization failure errors that the application can retry.

(Caveats: DDL operations do acquire stronger locks, SERIALIZABLE isolation can cause serialization failures on reads, and long-running transactions can block autovacuum and cause table bloat. MVCC is not contention-free, but the contention profile is very different from the lock-heavy model the book describes.)

This is not BASE. This is full ACID with MVCC: you get consistency and high concurrency. The trade-off the book describes (you must choose between consistency and performance) simply doesn’t exist in PostgreSQL’s architecture.

Furthermore, PostgreSQL offers configurable isolation levels for the rare cases where you want to trade some consistency for performance:

-- Read Committed (default): each statement sees committed data as of statement start
-- Repeatable Read: all statements in the transaction see a consistent snapshot
-- Serializable: full serializability via SSI (Serializable Snapshot Isolation)

-- You can choose per-transaction:
BEGIN ISOLATION LEVEL SERIALIZABLE;
-- Critical financial operations here
COMMIT;

BEGIN ISOLATION LEVEL READ COMMITTED;
-- Analytics queries that tolerate phantom reads
COMMIT;

This is more sophisticated than the binary ACID/BASE choice the book presents. You can use Serializable isolation for financial transfers and Read Committed for dashboard queries, within the same database, without any structural changes.

But what about eventually consistent reads at global scale?

Fair point. If you have users in Tokyo and New York both writing to the same data, and you need sub-10ms reads for both, you either need multi-region replication with eventual consistency or you need to accept cross-region latency. This is a genuine physics constraint, not a database design choice.

But here’s what the book gets wrong: this trade-off applies to any database, including NoSQL systems. Cassandra with cross-datacenter quorum reads has the same latency problem (and in practice, most multi-region deployments use LOCAL_QUORUM to avoid it, strongly consistent within one datacenter, but giving up cross-DC consistency). DynamoDB global tables have the same eventual consistency window. The choice isn’t “RDBMS means ACID means slow” vs. “NoSQL means BASE means fast.” The choice is “how much consistency are you willing to sacrifice for how much latency reduction, and at what geographic distance?”

PostgreSQL gives you tools for this too: logical replication to remote read replicas, asynchronous streaming for low-latency reads with slight lag, synchronous replication for strong consistency with higher latency. You make the trade-off explicitly, per-replication-target, rather than having it baked into your database engine’s fundamental architecture.

The hidden cost of BASE the book doesn’t discuss:

The 2013 analysis presents eventual consistency as a feature. In practice, it’s a liability that creates an entire category of bugs. A user creates an account, immediately tries to log in, and gets “account not found” because the write hasn’t replicated. A customer places an order, checks the status, and sees nothing because the read hit a stale replica. Two users update the same document at once, and one update is silently lost. Inventory shows 5 items available, 10 customers buy one each, and you’re 5 items oversold.

Every eventually consistent system requires application-level fixes for these scenarios: read-your-writes consistency, conflict resolution logic, anti-entropy processes, version vectors. The book mentions none of this. The “hammock on the beach” illustration for BASE is misleading. The hammock comes with a pager that goes off every time a consistency bug hits production.

I find this thoroughly debunked. The ACID/BASE trade-off as presented in the book is a false dilemma. PostgreSQL’s MVCC provides ACID guarantees with high concurrency. The genuine trade-offs at global scale apply equally to all databases, and PostgreSQL provides configurable tools to navigate them. The hidden engineering costs of eventual consistency are consistently understated.

Claim 4: “Joins don’t scale. NoSQL systems avoid them for better performance”

The argument: RDBMSs don’t scale out when joins are required. NoSQL systems allow you to extract your data using simple interfaces without joins.

The reality in 2026:

This is the most technically interesting claim in the book, and the one with the most nuance.

The kernel of truth: Distributed joins across network boundaries are expensive. If table A lives on node 1 and table B lives on node 2, joining them requires shipping data across the network. This is genuinely slower than a local join on a single node, and it’s genuinely harder to optimize.

The logical error: The book concludes that because distributed joins are hard, the solution is to avoid joins entirely. This is like saying “because stairs are hard to climb with a broken leg, we should eliminate stairs from all buildings.” The correct response is to fix the leg, not to redesign the building.

What joins actually cost on a single node:

On a single PostgreSQL instance (which, remember, handles the vast majority of workloads), joins are highly optimized:

-- This join is essentially free with proper indexes
SELECT o.id, o.total, c.name, c.email
FROM orders o
JOIN customers c ON c.id = o.customer_id
WHERE o.created_at > now() - interval '24 hours';

-- With indexes on orders(created_at) and customers(id), this executes in
-- milliseconds even with millions of rows. The join is a simple index lookup.

PostgreSQL’s query planner chooses between nested loop joins, hash joins, and merge joins based on table statistics. For indexed foreign key lookups (the most common join pattern), the cost is effectively one additional index lookup per row, which is nanoseconds on modern hardware.

The “joins don’t scale” claim is only true when:

  1. The tables being joined live on different physical machines, AND
  2. The join requires shipping large intermediate result sets across the network, AND
  3. The query can’t be restructured to filter before joining.

For the standard OLTP pattern (“fetch an order with its customer details”), even distributed joins are fast, because you’re joining on an indexed key with high selectivity.

The cost of avoiding joins (denormalization):

The NoSQL approach to avoiding joins is denormalization, embedding related data within each document:

{
    "order_id": 12345,
    "total": 99.99,
    "customer": {
        "name": "Alice Johnson",
        "email": "alice@example.com",
        "address": "123 Main St"
    },
    "items": [
        {"product": "Widget", "price": 49.99, "qty": 2}
    ]
}

This trades read-time joins for:

  • Write amplification: When Alice updates her email, you must update it in every order document that embeds her data. With 10,000 orders, that’s 10,000 writes instead of one.
  • Data inconsistency windows: Unless you wrap every denormalization update in a multi-document transaction (which MongoDB has supported since 4.0, with sharded-cluster support in 4.2, though with performance overhead that many teams avoid at scale), some documents show the old email and some show the new one between the first and last update. You’ve introduced eventual consistency at the application level even if your database supports strong consistency.
  • Storage bloat: Alice’s name, email, and address are stored 10,000 times instead of once. At scale, this adds up to significant storage and I/O costs.
  • Update anomalies: If your denormalization update fails partway through (application crash, network issue) and you’re not using multi-document transactions, your data is silently inconsistent with no database-level mechanism to detect or repair it.

Normalization isn’t just academic database theory. It’s an engineering technique that reduces write amplification, prevents update anomalies, and saves storage. Joins are the mechanism that makes normalization practical. Removing joins doesn’t remove the need for data relationships; it just pushes the relationship management into your application code, where it’s harder to get right, harder to test, and impossible for the database to optimize.

This is the claim I find most interesting to dissect, and it’s mostly debunked. Joins on a single node are fast and well-optimized. Distributed joins have real costs, but the alternative (denormalization) has costs too, costs that the NoSQL literature systematically ignores. For the workloads that actually need distributed queries, NewSQL systems (CockroachDB, Spanner) provide distributed joins with acceptable performance. The “avoid joins” advice optimizes for one narrow scenario while creating problems in every other scenario.

Claim 5: “You need NoSQL for full-text search”

The argument: Full-text search requires third-party tools in RDBMS systems. NoSQL systems provide integrated search functions that provide high-quality ranked search results.

The reality in 2026:

This claim was wrong when the book was published, and it’s still wrong now.

PostgreSQL has had built-in full-text search since version 8.3 (2008). Six years before the book was published. The implementation is mature and feature-rich:

-- Create a search-optimized column
ALTER TABLE articles ADD COLUMN search_vector tsvector
    GENERATED ALWAYS AS (
        setweight(to_tsvector('english', coalesce(title, '')), 'A') ||
        setweight(to_tsvector('english', coalesce(body, '')), 'B')
    ) STORED;

-- Index it
CREATE INDEX idx_articles_search ON articles USING gin(search_vector);

-- Search with ranking
SELECT title, ts_rank(search_vector, query) AS rank
FROM articles, to_tsquery('english', 'database & scaling') AS query
WHERE search_vector @@ query
ORDER BY rank DESC
LIMIT 20;

PostgreSQL’s full-text search supports language-aware stemming (“running” matches “run”), weighted fields (title matches rank higher than body), phrase and prefix search, custom dictionaries, result highlighting via ts_headline(), and fuzzy matching through the pg_trgm extension.

Is it Elasticsearch? No. Elasticsearch offers more advanced relevance tuning, better faceted search, and native distributed indexing. If search is your core product feature (you’re building a search engine, an e-commerce product discovery experience, or a log analytics platform), Elasticsearch (or OpenSearch, or Typesense, or Meilisearch) earns its place.

But for the 90% case (“add a search bar to your application”), PostgreSQL’s built-in search is more than adequate, and it has a decisive advantage: your search index is transactionally consistent with your data. When you insert a row, the search index updates atomically within the same transaction. No replication lag, no eventual consistency, no separate cluster to manage.

The moment you add Elasticsearch alongside PostgreSQL, you inherit two systems to maintain, a synchronization pipeline that can fail, an eventual consistency window where search results don’t reflect recent writes, double the infrastructure cost, and a new class of bugs where the search index disagrees with the source of truth.

Debunked. If you’re adding search to an existing app, try PostgreSQL’s built-in FTS before reaching for Elasticsearch. Dedicated search engines earn their complexity only when search is a core product differentiator.

Claim 6: “Object-relational mapping is a fundamental barrier to agility”

The argument: The most complex part of building applications using RDBMSs is the object-relational mapping layer. NoSQL document stores eliminate this complexity because documents can be stored and retrieved directly without translation.

The reality in 2026:

The book describes a four-step translation process: HTML to middle-tier objects, objects to SQL, SQL results back to objects, objects back to HTML. It argues this is inherently complex and that document stores eliminate it.

The ORM complaint was legitimate in the era of heavyweight frameworks like Hibernate (Java) and NHibernate (.NET), where mapping configuration files could be longer than the application code they served. But the book mistakes a tooling problem for a structural problem.

In 2026, the tooling landscape looks nothing like 2013:

Approach 1: Type-safe SQL generation (no ORM needed)

// sqlc: write SQL, get type-safe Go code
// query.sql:
// -- name: GetOrderWithCustomer :one
// SELECT o.id, o.total, c.name, c.email
// FROM orders o JOIN customers c ON c.id = o.customer_id
// WHERE o.id = $1;

// Generated Go code -- no mapping, no reflection, no runtime overhead
order, err := queries.GetOrderWithCustomer(ctx, orderID)
// order.ID, order.Total, order.Name, order.Email are typed fields

Approach 2: Lightweight query builders

# SQLAlchemy Core (not the ORM) -- Python
stmt = (
    select(orders.c.id, orders.c.total, customers.c.name)
    .join(customers, orders.c.customer_id == customers.c.id)
    .where(orders.c.id == order_id)
)
result = conn.execute(stmt).fetchone()

Approach 3: PostgreSQL returning JSON directly

-- Skip the mapping entirely -- return JSON from the database
SELECT json_build_object(
    'id', o.id,
    'total', o.total,
    'customer', json_build_object('name', c.name, 'email', c.email)
) AS order_json
FROM orders o
JOIN customers c ON c.id = o.customer_id
WHERE o.id = $1;

That last approach is particularly devastating to the book’s argument. PostgreSQL can return fully structured JSON documents from normalized relational data in a single query. You get the storage efficiency and consistency of normalization with the API convenience of document stores. No ORM, no mapping layer, no translation steps.

The document store “impedance match” is also overstated.

The book claims that document stores provide a natural fit between application objects and database storage. But document stores have their own mapping problems:

  • Validation: Without a schema, invalid documents are silently accepted. You need application-level validation for every write.
  • Migration: When your document structure changes, you must handle old and new formats simultaneously. There’s no ALTER TABLE equivalent; you write migration code.
  • References: When documents reference other documents, you handle the resolution in application code. Every customer_id field in a MongoDB document is a manual join waiting to happen.
  • Aggregation: Complex queries across denormalized documents require aggregation pipelines that are often harder to write and debug than equivalent SQL.

The ORM problem was real in 2008. In 2026, it’s been solved by better tooling, not by changing the database model.

I consider this debunked. Modern SQL tooling (sqlc, Prisma, jOOQ, SQLAlchemy Core) and PostgreSQL’s native JSON functions eliminate the ORM burden without sacrificing relational guarantees. The book’s four-translation-step model describes a specific technology stack (Java/Hibernate/RDBMS), not a fundamental constraint of the relational model.

BTW. Avoid using ORMs in general =D!

Claim 7: “Key-value stores are the right choice for caching and simple lookups”

The argument: Key-value stores provide a simple put/get/delete interface that enables precision service levels, lower operational costs, and high scalability.

The reality in 2026:

This is the claim in the book that has aged the best, and it deserves honest treatment.

Where the book is right: Redis (or Valkey, its open-source fork post-license-change) remains the gold standard for:

  • Application caching: Sub-millisecond reads for hot data. PostgreSQL can’t match Redis for pure read throughput on cached data.
  • Session storage: Ephemeral data that benefits from TTL expiration.
  • Rate limiting: Atomic increment operations with expiration.
  • Pub/sub and queues: Redis Streams and pub/sub are mature, fast, and purpose-built.
  • Distributed locks: Redlock for coordination across application instances (though its safety guarantees have been shown to break under clock skew, so evaluate carefully for correctness-critical use cases).

For these use cases, Redis isn’t just better than PostgreSQL; it’s a different category of tool. Redis operates on data in RAM with optional persistence; PostgreSQL operates on persistent data with extensive caching. They serve different access patterns.

Where the book overstates the case:

The book suggests key-value stores for scenarios that PostgreSQL handles well:

  • Lookup tables: A PostgreSQL UNLOGGED TABLE with a primary key index provides sub-millisecond lookups and doesn’t require a separate system (note: unlogged tables are truncated on crash, so use them only for data you can reconstruct).
  • Configuration storage: hstore or jsonb columns with indexed keys are transactionally consistent and don’t need a cache invalidation strategy.
  • Blob storage: The book suggests key-value stores for images and binary files. In 2026, object storage (S3, GCS, R2) is the correct answer for blobs, not Redis, not PostgreSQL, and not the key-value stores the book describes.

The caching trap:

The book doesn’t discuss the most significant problem with caching layers: cache invalidation. The moment you put Redis between your application and PostgreSQL, you’ve introduced:

  • A consistency problem: how long can the cache serve stale data?
  • An invalidation problem: which writes should bust which cache keys?
  • A cold-start problem: what happens after a Redis restart?
  • A thundering-herd problem: when a popular cache key expires and hundreds of concurrent requests simultaneously hit the database to rebuild it?

These are well-understood problems with well-known solutions (cache-aside, write-through, TTL-based expiration). But they add real complexity, and for many applications, the simpler architecture (just query PostgreSQL with properly sized shared_buffers and a connection pooler like PgBouncer) provides adequate performance without the cache layer.

Partially valid, and I say that with appreciation for Redis. It’s a genuinely useful tool for specific access patterns. But the book overgeneralizes, suggesting key-value stores for workloads that PostgreSQL handles natively. The operational cost of maintaining a separate caching tier is consistently understated.

Claim 8: “Graph databases are essential for relationship-heavy data”

The argument: Graph stores are important for applications that need to analyze relationships between objects or visit all nodes in a graph. They are useful for social networking, fraud detection, creating mashups, and analyzing complex network structures.

The reality in 2026:

This is the one area where specialized NoSQL databases retain the strongest case, even if 3 out of 10 candidates in tech interviews suggest Neo4j for problems that a JOIN would solve. The boundary of when you actually need a graph database has shifted significantly.

What PostgreSQL handles now:

PostgreSQL’s recursive CTEs can express graph queries:

-- Find all direct and transitive reports for a manager
WITH RECURSIVE org_chart AS (
    SELECT id, name, manager_id, 1 AS depth
    FROM employees
    WHERE id = 42  -- starting manager

    UNION ALL

    SELECT e.id, e.name, e.manager_id, oc.depth + 1
    FROM employees e
    JOIN org_chart oc ON e.manager_id = oc.id
    WHERE oc.depth < 10  -- prevent infinite loops
)
SELECT * FROM org_chart ORDER BY depth, name;

For 1-3 hop queries on graphs with millions of nodes, PostgreSQL with proper indexes performs well. This covers:

  • Organizational hierarchies
  • Category trees
  • Bill-of-materials
  • Simple friend-of-friend queries
  • Dependency graphs

Additionally, the Apache AGE extension brings Cypher (Neo4j’s query language) to PostgreSQL:

-- Using Apache AGE for graph queries in PostgreSQL
SELECT * FROM cypher('social_graph', $$
    MATCH (a:Person {name: 'Alice'})-[:FRIENDS_WITH*1..3]-(b:Person)
    RETURN DISTINCT b.name, b.age
$$) AS (name agtype, age agtype);

This gives you graph query syntax with PostgreSQL’s storage engine, ACID guarantees, and the ability to join graph results with relational data.

Where specialized graph databases still win:

  • Deep traversals (4+ hops): Finding all paths between two nodes in a social graph with hundreds of millions of edges. Neo4j’s index-free adjacency gives it effectively O(1) per hop on a single machine (versus an index lookup per hop in PostgreSQL), while PostgreSQL’s recursive CTEs scale linearly with the number of edges visited.
  • Graph algorithms: PageRank, community detection, betweenness centrality, shortest path. These are built into Neo4j (GDS library) and would be too expensive to implement in SQL.
  • Real-time fraud detection: Traversing transaction graphs to detect circular patterns, velocity anomalies, and network-based risk scoring, where microsecond traversal times matter.
  • Knowledge graphs and ontologies: RDF (Resource Description Framework) stores with SPARQL support for linked data applications, semantic search, and reasoning.

The honest assessment:

If your graph queries are “show me who’s connected to X within 2-3 hops,” PostgreSQL (possibly with Apache AGE) is sufficient and dramatically simpler to operate. If your product is the graph (you’re building a social network’s feed algorithm, a knowledge graph platform, or a real-time fraud detection system), Neo4j or a similar purpose-built graph database earns its place.

The book treats graph databases as necessary for any application with relationships. In reality, all data has relationships; that’s what foreign keys express. Graph databases are necessary only when the traversal of deep, complex relationships is itself the primary workload.

Partially valid, but significantly overstated. Graph databases retain clear advantages for deep traversals and graph algorithms. But the threshold for needing one is much higher than the book suggests, and Apache AGE on PostgreSQL covers a substantial middle ground.

Claim 9: “Column-family stores are needed for big data analytics”

The argument: Column-family (Bigtable) stores provide a sparse matrix approach where users can think of all data as being stored in a single table with billions of rows and millions of columns. They’re essential for web crawling, large sparsely populated tables, and highly-adaptable systems.

The reality in 2026:

The analytical database landscape has transformed beyond recognition since 2014. The book’s recommendation of HBase and Cassandra for analytical workloads is outdated.

What actually happened:

Columnar storage won, but not in the way the book predicted. The winners weren’t column-family NoSQL stores; they were purpose-built columnar analytics engines like ClickHouse, DuckDB, Apache Druid, and BigQuery. Cassandra found its niche in write-heavy workloads (time-series, IoT, messaging), not analytics. HBase is effectively legacy, declining alongside the Hadoop ecosystem it depends on.

For moderate-scale analytics (hundreds of gigabytes to low terabytes), PostgreSQL itself is surprisingly capable. Parallel query execution, time-based partitioning with pruning, materialized views, BRIN indexes for naturally ordered data, and full window functions (RANK(), LAG(), LEAD(), running totals) cover a wide range of analytical needs on a single node.

When PostgreSQL isn’t enough, the answer in 2026 isn’t column-family NoSQL. It’s purpose-built OLAP engines: DuckDB for embedded analytics, ClickHouse for petabyte-scale real-time queries, Apache Druid for event-driven data, or managed options like BigQuery, Snowflake, and Redshift. None of these are “NoSQL” in the sense the book uses the term. They all support SQL. The industry learned that the right approach isn’t to abandon SQL, but to build storage engines optimized for analytical access patterns while keeping the query language everyone knows.

Obsolete. If you need analytics beyond what PostgreSQL handles, look at OLAP engines, not column-family stores. Cassandra retains value for write-heavy time-series workloads, but that’s a different use case than what the book describes.

Claim 10: “MapReduce is the key to distributed data processing”

The argument: Google’s MapReduce system is one of the most influential case studies in the NoSQL movement. By using parallel processing, indexing billions of web pages can be done quickly with commodity processors. MapReduce and distributed filesystems are the foundation for big data processing.

The reality in 2026:

MapReduce is dead for most practical purposes. Even Hadoop’s creators have moved on. The book captures MapReduce at its 2010-2014 peak, but Apache Spark (2014) offered 10-100x faster in-memory processing, cloud data warehouses (BigQuery GA in 2011, Redshift in 2013, Snowflake in 2015) replaced the Hadoop stack, and DuckDB (2019) proved that a single laptop can process the analytical workloads most organizations previously sent to Hadoop clusters.

In 2026, every workload the book attributes to MapReduce has a better answer: Spark or dbt for ETL, Flink or Kafka Streams for real-time processing, SQL on columnar engines for analytics. The irony is that Google itself deprecated MapReduce internally years ago in favor of Dataflow (which became Apache Beam). The paradigm the book presents as the future was already being phased out by its creator while the book was being written.

Obsolete, plain and simple. Historically significant, but zero relevance as a practical recommendation in 2026.

Claim 11: “NoSQL increases developer agility by eliminating the data modeling step”

The argument: NoSQL solutions can reduce the time it takes to build, scale, and modify applications. Loading test data can be done with drag-and-drop tools before ER modeling is complete. Schema-less datasets don’t require predefined tables, columns, or primary-foreign key relationships, making them more adaptable to change.

The reality in 2026:

This is perhaps the most seductive and most dangerous claim in the book.

The seduction: It’s true that you can start building with MongoDB faster than with PostgreSQL if your definition of “start” is “insert the first document.” No schema definition, no migration files, no up-front modeling. Just throw JSON at the database and go.

The danger: The book confuses deferring data modeling with eliminating the need for data modeling. You haven’t eliminated schema complexity; you’ve moved it from the database (where it’s explicit, validated, and documented) into your application code (where it’s implicit, unvalidated, and undocumented).

Every document database application eventually develops:

  1. Implicit schemas: “The user collection expects documents with name (string), email (string), and created_at (ISO 8601 date).” That is a schema. It just lives in a wiki page instead of a CREATE TABLE statement.

  2. Application-level validation: Since the database won’t reject invalid documents, every write path must validate the document structure. This validation code must be maintained in every service that writes to the collection. If one service misses a validation rule, the data is silently corrupted.

  3. Migration complexity: When the implicit schema changes (rename a field, change a type, restructure nested data), you face a choice:

    • Migrate all existing documents (write a script that reads and rewrites every document, with no transactional guarantee across the collection).
    • Support both old and new formats forever (the if doc.has_key('old_field') approach that gradually turns your code into an archaeological dig).

Compare this to PostgreSQL:

-- Schema change with zero downtime
ALTER TABLE users ADD COLUMN phone text;

-- With a default value -- metadata-only, instant, no table rewrite
ALTER TABLE users ADD COLUMN is_active boolean NOT NULL DEFAULT true;

-- Rename a column -- metadata-only, instant
ALTER TABLE users RENAME COLUMN name TO full_name;

-- The database enforces the new schema for all future writes.
-- No application code changes needed for validation.
-- All clients see the new schema immediately.

The agility comparison in the book is stacked.

Table 9.1 assigns 4 “effort units” to the NoSQL approach and 29 to SQL/Java, a comparison that reflects 2013 workflows and significantly understates modern SQL productivity:

  1. It compares the worst possible SQL workflow against the best possible NoSQL workflow. The SQL approach uses manual JDBC programming in Java. Nobody does this in 2026. With modern tooling (sqlc, Prisma, FastAPI + SQLAlchemy), the effort for the SQL approach would be comparable.

  2. It front-loads all NoSQL costs to zero. The “drag and drop XML into collection” step assumes no validation, no indexing strategy, no access control, and no thought about query patterns. All of these costs exist; they’re just deferred to later, when they’re more expensive to address.

  3. It ignores ongoing maintenance costs. The NoSQL approach is cheaper on day 1. But over a 3-year project lifecycle, the costs of schema drift, migration complexity, and application-level validation frequently exceed the up-front cost of proper data modeling.

The real agility equation:

True agility isn’t about how fast you can insert the first record. It’s about how fast you can safely modify a production system under load. PostgreSQL’s transactional DDL, online schema migrations (with tools like pgroll or pg-osc), and constraint-based validation make it more agile for production operations than schema-less databases, where every change requires careful analysis of existing document variations.

None of this means the appeal was fake. The NoSQL movement succeeded on this point because removing CREATE TABLE from the day-0 workflow genuinely feels faster, and it is faster, on day 0. The costs arrive, with interest, on day 365.

I’ve seen this pattern play out too many times to call it anything but debunked. Eliminating data modeling doesn’t increase agility; it shifts complexity from explicit, database-enforced schemas to implicit, application-enforced conventions. The former is visible, testable, and self-documenting. The latter is a source of bugs, tech debt, and operational incidents. The book’s agility comparison reflects 2013 tooling and doesn’t account for how much modern SQL workflows have improved.

Claim 12: “Native XML databases are the future for document management”

The argument: They dedicates 28 pages to native XML databases, XQuery, XPath, XML Schema, and Schematron, presenting MarkLogic as a leading NoSQL solution and XRX (XForms + REST + XQuery) as a web architecture.

The reality in 2026:

JSON won (liking it or not). Comprehensively, decisively, and irreversibly. MarkLogic pivoted to “multi-model database” marketing. The W3C dissolved the XForms Working Group in 2015. XQuery survives only in legacy systems. XML Schema has been replaced by JSON Schema, OpenAPI, and Protobuf. PostgreSQL’s jsonb support made the entire XML discussion moot for new projects. If you’re evaluating databases in 2026, skip Chapter 5.

Part III: where specialized databases still earn their place

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that PostgreSQL is not the answer to everything. Here’s my clear-eyed assessment of when specialized databases are the right choice in 2026:

Redis / Valkey: sub-millisecond in-memory operations

Use when: You need a caching layer, session store, rate limiter, distributed lock manager, or pub/sub message broker. Redis’s data structures (sorted sets, streams, HyperLogLog) solve specific problems more elegantly than any SQL-based approach.

Don’t use when: You’re using it as a primary data store for data you can’t afford to lose, or when PostgreSQL with proper indexing and PgBouncer provides adequate latency.

Apache Kafka: distributed event streaming

Use when: You need durable, ordered, replayable event streams at high throughput (I said high, and not 500k RPM). Kafka is infrastructure, not a database, and serves as the backbone for event-driven architectures, CDC pipelines, and stream processing.

Don’t use when: You need a message queue (use a simpler tool like NATS or RabbitMQ) or when PostgreSQL’s LISTEN/NOTIFY covers your pub/sub needs.

ClickHouse: real-time analytics at scale

Use when: You have billions of rows of event data and need sub-second analytical queries. ClickHouse’s columnar storage and vectorized execution engine is 10-100x faster than PostgreSQL for full-table aggregations on large datasets.

Don’t use when: Your analytical dataset fits comfortably in PostgreSQL with proper partitioning and materialized views (i.e., under ~500GB with reasonable query patterns).

Neo4j (or similiar): deep graph traversals

Use when: Your core workload involves multi-hop graph traversals (4+ hops), graph algorithms (PageRank, community detection, shortest path), or knowledge graph reasoning. Neo4j’s index-free adjacency and GDS library are purpose-built for these patterns.

Don’t use when: Your “graph” queries are 1-3 hop lookups that PostgreSQL’s recursive CTEs or Apache AGE handle efficiently.

DynamoDB: predictable performance at planetary scale

Use when: You need single-digit millisecond read/write latency at any scale, with no capacity planning, fully managed, and you’re willing to accept a key-value + secondary index query model. DynamoDB is arguably the best-operated database service on any cloud.

Don’t use when: You need ad-hoc queries, joins, or flexible access patterns. DynamoDB requires you to design your access patterns up front and model your data around them. Getting this wrong is expensive to fix.

ScyllaDB / Cassandra: extreme write throughput

Use when: You need to sustain millions of writes per second across a distributed cluster, with tunable consistency and multi-datacenter replication. IoT sensor data, messaging systems, and time-series at extreme scale.

Don’t use when: Your write throughput is under 50k simple writes/second (PostgreSQL handles this on a single well-tuned node), or when you need strong consistency and ad-hoc queries.

Elasticsearch / OpenSearch: search as a core product feature

Use when: Search quality, relevance tuning, faceted navigation, and full-text search are core to your product experience. E-commerce product search, log analytics (ELK stack), enterprise search.

Don’t use when: You need a search bar on your application and PostgreSQL’s tsvector + pg_trgm provides adequate results.

Vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant): semantic search and AI workloads

Use when: Your core workload is high-dimensional similarity search at scale: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipelines, recommendation engines, image similarity, or semantic search over millions of embeddings where you need sub-10ms query latency with advanced filtering and metadata support.

Don’t use when: PostgreSQL with pgvector covers your needs. pgvector supports approximate nearest-neighbor indexes (HNSW for high recall, IVFFlat for faster builds with less memory), handles millions of vectors on a single node, and keeps your embeddings transactionally consistent with your data. For most applications adding semantic search to an existing product, pgvector is enough. The same principle applies: start with PostgreSQL, add a specialized system when you have measured evidence.

SQLite / Turso: edge and embedded workloads

Use when: Your application benefits from an embedded database with zero operational overhead (mobile apps, desktop software, CLI tools, or edge deployments) where a client-server database is impractical. Turso (built on libSQL, a fork of SQLite) extends this model to multi-tenant edge applications with per-tenant databases and replication. The “database per user” pattern that would be too expensive with PostgreSQL becomes natural with SQLite.

Don’t use when: You need concurrent writes from multiple processes, complex transactions, or the extension ecosystem that PostgreSQL provides. SQLite is single-writer by design: brilliant for read-heavy edge workloads, wrong for multi-service backends.

Part IV: a decision framework for 2026

Instead of the book’s “pick a NoSQL category” approach, here’s how I think about database choices in 2026:

Step 1: start with PostgreSQL

This is not tribal loyalty. It’s engineering economics. PostgreSQL handles relational, document, key-value, full-text search, time-series (with TimescaleDB), and basic graph workloads. It provides ACID transactions that eliminate entire categories of application bugs. It has the most mature ecosystem of extensions, tools, and managed service providers. And it scales vertically to workloads that cover 99% of applications. One database to monitor, backup, upgrade, and hire for.

PostgreSQL is not without operational pain. VACUUM and table bloat require monitoring and tuning, especially on write-heavy tables. High-concurrency workloads need a connection pooler (PgBouncer or PgCat). Major version upgrades via pg_upgrade may require brief downtime (though --link mode often completes in seconds), or careful logical replication cutover for zero-downtime requirements.

These are real costs, but they’re well-understood costs with mature tooling, and they apply to a single system rather than being multiplied across a constellation of databases.

Starting with PostgreSQL isn’t a permanent commitment. It’s a default that you override with evidence, not speculation. I’ve started every greenfield project this way for the past several years, and I haven’t regretted it once.

Step 2: identify concrete bottlenecks, not theoretical ones

Don’t add a database because you might need it someday. Add it when you have a specific, measured problem:

SymptomMeasurementLikely Solution
Read latency too high for hot datap99 > 10ms for cached lookupsRedis caching layer
Analytical queries too slowFull-table scans on 500M+ rows taking minutesClickHouse or DuckDB
Write throughput saturatedWAL write latency increasing, replication lag growingCitus sharding, or ScyllaDB for extreme cases
Graph traversals too slowRecursive CTEs too slow at 4+ hops on 100M+ edgesNeo4j for the graph workload
Search quality inadequateUser complaints, low click-through on search resultsElasticsearch for search
Need multi-region active-activeBusiness requirement for <50ms writes globallyCockroachDB or Spanner
Semantic search at scalepgvector recall/latency insufficient at your embedding volumePinecone, Weaviate, or Qdrant

Step 3: add specialized databases as satellites, not replacements

When you do add a specialized database, PostgreSQL remains the system of record:

                    ┌──────────────┐
                    │  PostgreSQL  │
                    │  (source of  │
                    │   truth)     │
                    └──────┬───────┘
                           │
              ┌────────────┼────────────┐
              │            │            │
        ┌─────▼────┐ ┌─────▼─────┐  ┌────▼──────┐
        │  Redis   │ │ClickHouse │  │Elastic    │
        │ (cache)  │ │(analytics)│  │(search)   │
        └──────────┘ └───────────┘  └───────────┘

Data flows from PostgreSQL to specialized systems via CDC (Change Data Capture) or event streams. PostgreSQL enforces integrity; specialized systems provide optimized read paths. This is CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation) at the data layer: one source of truth for writes, purpose-built stores for reads. That’s far simpler than trying to coordinate writes across multiple database engines.

Step 4: evaluate operational cost honestly

Every database you add to your architecture incurs infrastructure cost (servers, storage, backups), operational cost (monitoring, upgrades, security patches), and cognitive cost (your team must understand the data model, failure modes, and runbooks for each system). On top of that, keeping data synchronized across systems requires pipelines that can fail, lag, or lose data. And finding engineers who know PostgreSQL is easy. Finding engineers who know PostgreSQL and Cassandra and Elasticsearch and Neo4j is expensive.

A system with one database (PostgreSQL) and adequate performance beats a system with four databases and perfect performance in almost every engineering dimension: reliability, debuggability, time-to-market, and operational cost.

Part V: what the book got right that still matters

Dismantling the book’s technical recommendations is easy with thirteen years of hindsight. Harder, and more useful, is identifying the ideas that survived the technology churn. I appreciate that several of the book’s underlying principles remain valuable precisely because they’re independent of any specific database product.

1. The CAP theorem as a thinking tool (not a selection tool)

The book’s explanation of CAP is actually quite good. It correctly describes the theorem as applying during network partitions, not during normal operation, which is more sophisticated than many treatments of the topic. The problem isn’t the explanation but the application: the book goes on to use CAP as a database selection tool, which is a leap the theorem doesn’t support.

What I find more useful today is PACELC, Daniel Abadi’s extension of CAP. The idea is simple: during a Partition, you choose between Availability and Consistency (that’s CAP), but Else (normal operation), you choose between Latency and Consistency. That “else” clause is what matters 99% of the time, and it’s exactly what CAP leaves out. Most database decisions aren’t about partition tolerance. They’re about how much latency you’ll accept for how much consistency during normal, healthy operation. PACELC gives you a better vocabulary for those everyday trade-offs.

2. The importance of understanding your access patterns

The book’s emphasis on matching data architecture to business problems is timeless. The mistake wasn’t in recommending analysis; it was in assuming the analysis would always conclude that a non-relational database was needed. The trade-off framework (quality trees, stakeholder communication) is sound methodology applied to a now-outdated technology landscape.

3. The value of simplicity in system design

The discussion of keeping components simple and using UNIX-pipe-style modularity is genuinely good advice. I find that this advice now argues for PostgreSQL: a single, well-understood database is simpler than a constellation of purpose-built stores, each with its own operational model.

4. The recognition that one size doesn’t fit all

McCreary and Kelly repeatedly emphasize that NoSQL isn’t a replacement for SQL, but an augmentation. The “Sally Solutions” case studies show a solution architect recommending SQL for some problems and NoSQL for others. This pragmatism was ahead of the marketing hype that would follow the book’s publication, when “NoSQL” was used as a synonym for “better.”

Tthe post-NoSQL era

The NoSQL movement was not wrong about the problems. In 2010, many RDBMSs did struggle with horizontal scaling, schema flexibility, high-write throughput, and developer agility. The diagnosis was accurate.

The treatment was wrong. The conclusion “therefore, abandon SQL, ACID, and the relational model” was a logical leap that didn’t follow. The correct conclusion was “therefore, improve RDBMS implementations to address these limitations.” And that’s exactly what happened, not only with PostgreSQL, but with the entire NewSQL movement (CockroachDB, Spanner, TiDB, YugabyteDB) that provides distributed horizontal scaling with full SQL and ACID support.

The lasting contribution of the NoSQL movement isn’t any specific database product. It’s the pressure it applied to the relational database community to innovate: jsonb in PostgreSQL, JSON support in MySQL, multi-model capabilities across the board. The competition made relational databases better, which is the highest compliment the NoSQL movement can receive and the most damning assessment of its central thesis.

For most backend applications in 2026, my answer to “what database should I use?” is: start with PostgreSQL, measure your workload, and add specialized databases only when you have empirical evidence that PostgreSQL can’t meet specific, measured requirements. Not because PostgreSQL is perfect, but because the operational simplicity of running one database instead of five is worth more than the theoretical performance gains of purpose-built alternatives that you probably don’t need.

You can always add complexity later. You can never take it back. I believe that deeply.
