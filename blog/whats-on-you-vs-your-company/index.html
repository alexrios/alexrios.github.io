Part of series: Burnout

What's on you vs. what's on your company

27 December 2025 / 10 min read
  • leadership
  • burnout
  • management

When you’re burned out, you need to answer a question that nobody wants to answer: how much of this is the environment, and how much is me?

The answer matters because it determines what to do. If it’s the environment, you need to advocate for change or leave. If it’s you, you need to change how you operate. If it’s both—which it usually is—you need to work on both, and be honest about which is which.

Most burnout conversations skip this. They treat burnout as either a systemic problem (blame the company) or a personal one (blame yourself). Neither framing is complete. Burnout lives in the intersection.

Why the distinction matters

Consider two engineers with identical symptoms: exhaustion, cynicism, declining performance.

Engineer A is burned out because the company has unrealistic deadlines, no-win performance reviews, and a culture of constant firefighting. They’ve tried to set boundaries. They’ve pushed back on scope. Nothing changes. The environment is designed to grind people down.

Engineer B is burned out because they can’t stop overcommitting. They volunteer for everything. They feel guilty leaving at 6pm. They measure their worth by how indispensable they are. The company has reasonable expectations; they’ve created unreasonable ones for themselves.

Same symptoms. Completely different causes. Completely different solutions.

If A tries to fix themselves—meditation, better boundaries, therapy—they’ll feel marginally better while the environment continues to be unsustainable. The real fix is changing the environment or leaving it.

If B changes jobs, they’ll feel better for a few months, then recreate the same patterns. The real fix is changing how they relate to work.

Most burned-out people are some combination of A and B. The environment is genuinely difficult, and they’re also doing things that make it worse. Separating these is the hard work nobody wants to do.

The diagnostic framework

Maslach’s six areas of worklife provide a useful structure for diagnosing what’s organizational versus what’s individual.

For each factor, ask: is this a problem with my organization, or a problem with how I’m operating within it?

Workload

Organizational problem: The deadlines are genuinely impossible. The team is understaffed for the scope. On-call rotations are unsustainable. The workload would burn out anyone.

Individual problem: You’re adding work that nobody asked for. You’re gold-plating when good enough would suffice. You can’t prioritize ruthlessly. You feel guilty when you’re not maxed out.

Diagnostic question: If you did exactly what was required—no more, no less—would the workload still be unsustainable?

Control

Organizational problem: You have no input on decisions that affect your work. Requirements change without explanation. You’re told what to do and how to do it.

Individual problem: You need more control than the role provides. You can’t tolerate uncertainty. You try to control things outside your scope. You mistake “I wasn’t consulted” for “I was disrespected.”

Diagnostic question: Is your need for control reasonable for your role, or are you expecting autonomy that the position was never designed to offer?

Reward

Organizational problem: Good work goes unrecognized. Compensation is unfair. Promotions go to visibility, not impact. The system doesn’t reward what you value.

Individual problem: You need more recognition than is realistic. You discount private appreciation in favor of public praise. You’re never satisfied because the bar keeps moving.

Diagnostic question: Would a reasonable person feel recognized in your position, or is your need for reward outstripping what any job could provide?

Community

Organizational problem: The culture is toxic. Trust is broken. People undermine each other. There’s no psychological safety. The community is genuinely bad.

Individual problem: You struggle to connect. You keep people at arm’s length. You’re quick to interpret actions as hostile. You haven’t invested in relationships.

Diagnostic question: Do other people seem to have healthy relationships in this environment, or is it barren for everyone?

Fairness

Organizational problem: Decisions are arbitrary. Accountability is inconsistent. Politics matters more than performance. The system is genuinely unfair.

Individual problem: You’re hypersensitive to perceived slights. You see unfairness where others see reasonable judgment calls. You assume malice where there’s just messiness.

Diagnostic question: If you surveyed 10 reasonable people in your situation, would they agree that the unfairness is real?

Values

Organizational problem: The company says one thing and does another. You’re asked to cut corners that violate your ethics. The mission is hollow. The values mismatch is real.

Individual problem: Your values are unrealistic for the industry. You expect purity that no organization provides. You can’t compromise on anything without feeling like you’ve betrayed yourself.

Diagnostic question: Is the values gap specific to this company, or have you had the same conflict everywhere you’ve worked?

The honest audit

Here’s an exercise that hurts but helps.

For each of the six factors, write down:

  1. What the organization is doing that contributes to the problem
  2. What you are doing that contributes to the problem
  3. Your honest estimate of the split (70/30? 50/50? 90/10?)

Be brutal. Nobody else sees this list.

Most people find the split isn’t 100-0 in either direction. Maybe the workload is genuinely unsustainable (70% organizational), but you’re also volunteering for extra projects (30% individual). Maybe the culture is toxic (60% organizational), but you’re also quick to interpret things negatively (40% individual).

The percentages don’t have to be precise. They just force you to admit that both factors exist.

What you can change, what you can advocate for, what requires leaving

Once you’ve diagnosed the split, you can categorize your options.

What you can change (now)

These are the individual contributions—the patterns you control. You can start changing them immediately, without anyone’s permission.

  • Setting boundaries on your time
  • Saying no to voluntary commitments
  • Reducing your need for external validation
  • Letting go of perfectionism
  • Stopping the hero behavior
  • Accepting that some things will fail without your intervention

Changing these doesn’t require the organization’s cooperation. It might have consequences—maybe they reward the old patterns and punish the new ones—but the change itself is within your control.

Start here, even if the environment is bad. You’ll learn something. Either you’ll find that your patterns were a larger part of the problem than you thought, or you’ll confirm that the environment really does require unsustainable behavior to survive.

What you can advocate for (with effort)

These are organizational problems that could change if you push for change.

  • Making workload concerns visible to leadership
  • Proposing process improvements
  • Building coalitions with others who share the concern
  • Using your performance capital to push for better conditions
  • Escalating when decisions seem unfair

Advocacy takes energy. It doesn’t always work. But it’s the path between accepting the status quo and leaving. Some environments are genuinely responsive to advocacy. Some aren’t. You won’t know until you try.

The key is to be strategic. Don’t advocate for everything—focus on the factors that are most broken and most likely to improve. Document your concerns. Propose specific solutions. Build allies.

If advocacy works, the environment improves. If it doesn’t, you’ve learned something important: this organization doesn’t respond to feedback. That’s useful information for the next decision.

What requires leaving

Some problems can’t be solved by changing yourself or advocating for change.

The culture is toxic at the top. You can improve your corner, but you can’t fix a leadership team that creates dysfunction. The rot spreads faster than any individual can clean.

Your role is fundamentally designed to be unsustainable. Some positions are set up to burn through whoever holds them. If three predecessors all burned out, it’s not the people—it’s the role.

Your values and the company’s are irreconcilably opposed. You can compromise on details. You can’t compromise on fundamentals without eroding yourself.

You’ve advocated repeatedly and hit the same walls. Once is bad luck. Twice is a pattern. Three times is data. The organization has shown you who it is.

The accumulated damage is too deep to recover from while staying. Sometimes you’re too depleted to work on patterns while also surviving the environment. You need to escape before you can heal.

Your physical health is suffering. You can’t sleep. You’re stress-eating or not eating. You’re getting sick. Your body is telling you something. Listen.

You’re becoming someone you don’t like. You’re snapping at people. You’re cynical in ways that don’t feel like you. The job is changing who you are, not just how you feel.

Leaving is appropriate when the organizational factors are dominant and unchangeable. Not as a first resort—you should try changing your patterns and advocating for organizational change first. But as a clear-eyed acknowledgment that some environments will burn out anyone who stays long enough.

The trap is leaving too early (before you’ve addressed your own patterns) or too late (after the damage is done). There’s no formula for the right timing. But if you’ve done the honest audit, tried changing what you can change, and advocated without result—leaving stops being running away and starts being a rational response to an unsolvable problem.

Why wellness apps don’t fix structural problems

Here’s where organizations go wrong: they see burnout as an individual problem and offer individual solutions.

Meditation apps. Wellness stipends. Mental health days. Resilience training.

These are not bad things. But they’re Band-Aids on a systemic wound.

If the workload is genuinely unsustainable, a meditation app won’t fix it. If the culture is toxic, a mental health day means you’ll return to the same toxicity. If the reward system is broken, no amount of personal resilience will make it fair.

Wellness initiatives address the individual side of burnout. They do nothing for the organizational side. And when organizations offer only individual solutions, they’re implicitly saying: the environment is fine, you’re the problem.

This is sometimes true. But it’s often not. And when it’s not, wellness programs become a form of gaslighting. “We gave you the tools to handle stress—why are you still struggling?” The answer is that no individual tool can make an impossible situation possible.

If you work for a company that talks about wellness but won’t address workload, culture, or systemic unfairness—be skeptical. They’re treating symptoms while ignoring causes.

The intersection

Here’s the uncomfortable truth that neither side wants to hear:

To organizations: You can’t wellness-app your way out of a broken culture. If people are burning out, something about how you operate is causing it. Examine your workload expectations, your control structures, your reward systems, your community dynamics, your fairness, your values alignment. The problem isn’t that your people aren’t resilient enough.

To individuals: You can’t blame-the-company your way out of patterns you carry with you. If you’ve burned out multiple times in multiple places, something about how you operate is contributing. Examine your relationship with boundaries, validation, heroism, perfectionism. The problem isn’t that every company is toxic.

Burnout sits in the intersection. Both factors are usually present. Both factors usually contribute.

The work is to honestly assess each, change what you can on both sides, and make clear-eyed decisions about what’s fixable and what’s not.

This is hard. It requires honesty that’s uncomfortable. It means neither fully blaming the organization (which is satisfying) nor fully blaming yourself (which feels controllable). It means sitting in the complexity.

But complexity is where the real solutions live. Easy answers—it’s all them, or it’s all you—lead to the same place: burned out again, wondering why nothing ever changes.

Something can change. But it requires looking at both sides of the intersection, honestly, and deciding what to do about each.

Questions worth sitting with:

  • For each of the six factors, what’s your honest split between organizational and individual contribution?
  • What changes are you avoiding because they require you to do something differently?
  • If you fixed everything about yourself, would this environment still burn you out? If you fixed everything about the environment, would you still burn out?
